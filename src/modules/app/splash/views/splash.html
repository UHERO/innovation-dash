TRACKING HAWAII’S PROGRESS IN JOB GROWTH, BUSINESS CLIMATE AND ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT <BETA>

Innovation is the key to regional growth and prosperity. To better understand Hawaii’s progress in developing a vibrant innovation based economy this dashboard tracks key innovation assets and outcomes, and when possible, allows for comparison with other states and counties. The dashboard is supported by the Hawaii Business Roundtable, Devleague, and the University of Hawaii.

Education

Talented people are the source of innovation. Tracking measures of human capital helps gauge the availability of a skilled workforce, particularly in important technical fields.

In 2013 31.2% of Hawaii’s working age population held a Bachelor’s degree or higher versus 29.6% nationally.

For every 1000 individuals aged 25-34, Hawaii had

11.1

Science and Engineering graduate students in 2011 while the national average was 13.3.

Research &
Development

Research and Development (R&D) spending at universities and private R&D investment is critical for the accumulation of knowledge and developing new products and services that will drive regional growth and prosperity.

In 2012 Academic Science and Engineering R&D in Hawaii reached

$4.68

per $1000 of GDP vs $4.04 for the nation as a whole.

In 2012, Hawaii spent $328,168 on Academic Science and Engineering R&D.  And, Hawaii Business R&D as a share of private industry output, reached

4.8%

in 2011, more than double the US rate of 2.3%.

Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurial capacity determines Hawaii’s ability to generate new business activities through the development of new ideas and innovation.

As a share of GDP, Hawaii’s venture capital disbursements fall far below those in the rest of the country. In 2012, for every $1,000 of GDP, VC disbursements total

$.01

versus $1.73 nationally. This comparison should improve with the increased deal flow due to the growth in accelerators and Startup Paradise.

Hawaii’s economy has long been dominated by older firms. The share of workers employed by young firms (<5yrs old) or startups (<1yr old) in 2012 was 9.4%, less than half of the national share.

Economic
Prosperity

If innovation is the key to long run economic growth, the key outcome is higher living standards and economic prosperity. Hawaii growth has fallen behind the nation since the 1990s and living standards have suffered.

In 2013, average compensation per job in Hawaii was

$52,151

below the national average of $55,770.

Yet 2013 regional price parities show that, on average, Hawaii purchasing power is

16%

below the national average.
